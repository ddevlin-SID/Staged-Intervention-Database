ASN Resources

Practical information and resources to support learners with additional support needs in Scottish schools.

For information only. Always follow your school's policies and seek specialist advice when needed.

Overview

Autism is a lifelong neurodevelopmental difference that affects how a person communicates, interacts, and processes information. Every autistic learner is different. Some may need minimal support; others may need significant adjustments. Strengths can include attention to detail, logical thinking, and deep focus on areas of interest.

In the classroom

  • May struggle with change, transitions, or unexpected events
  • May find busy or noisy environments overwhelming (sensory overload)
  • May have difficulty reading social cues or understanding unwritten rules
  • May prefer clear, literal instructions over implied or abstract ones
  • May have intense interests they focus on
  • May use repetitive movements (stimming) to regulate

Suggested strategies

  • Use visual timetables and prepare for transitions in advance
  • Give clear, explicit instructions; avoid sarcasm or idioms
  • Consider seating (away from distractions, near a calm area)
  • Allow movement breaks or quiet time when needed
  • Be predictable and consistent with routines
  • Allow use of fidgets or stimming if it helps regulation

Further reading and CPD

  • National Autistic Society – Advice, guidance, and resources
  • Scottish Autism – Scotland’s leading autism charity; advice and training
  • The Autism Toolbox (Education Scotland) – Practical support for inclusion in mainstream schools

Overview

Dyslexia is a specific learning difficulty that mainly affects literacy skills (reading, writing, spelling). It is not linked to intelligence. Learners with dyslexia may take longer to process written text, mix up letter order, or struggle with phonics. Many have strengths in verbal reasoning, creativity, or problem-solving.

In the classroom

  • Slow or laboured reading; may avoid reading aloud
  • Spelling difficulties; inconsistent spelling of the same word
  • Struggle to take notes while listening
  • May need more time to complete written tasks
  • May find it hard to sequence or remember lists
  • Can tire quickly when doing literacy-heavy work

Suggested strategies

  • Use dyslexia-friendly fonts, spacing, and background colours
  • Provide printed notes or summaries instead of copying from the board
  • Allow extra time for reading and writing tasks
  • Use assistive technology (text-to-speech, speech-to-text)
  • Offer alternatives to written assessments where appropriate
  • Break instructions into small steps

Further reading and CPD

  • Addressing Dyslexia Toolkit – Free toolkit for identification, assessment, and support
  • Dyslexia and Inclusive Practice (GTC Scotland) – Professional Recognition programme
  • Dyslexia Scotland Online Learning – Free modules for teachers

Overview

ADHD (Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder) is a neurodevelopmental condition. Learners may have difficulty sustaining attention, struggle with impulse control, or show high levels of physical activity. Some have mainly inattentive presentation (ADD). ADHD is not a behaviour choice; it affects how the brain regulates attention and behaviour.

In the classroom

  • Difficulty focusing on tasks, especially sustained or less stimulating ones
  • May fidget, move, or leave their seat
  • May call out or interrupt; struggle to wait their turn
  • Can lose or forget equipment, homework, and instructions
  • May rush through work or miss steps
  • Time perception can be inconsistent

Suggested strategies

  • Seat near the teacher, away from distractions; use positive peers as models
  • Break tasks into small steps; one or two activities per page
  • Use visual timetables and predictable routines
  • Give clear, concise instructions; ask them to repeat back
  • Allow movement breaks and alternate active and sitting tasks
  • Use checklists and support organisation (desk, materials, homework)

Further reading and CPD

  • ADHD (Education Scotland) – Guidance for practitioners
  • ATTIC: ADHD in the Classroom – Tips and tricks for school staff
  • Enquire – Information on ASL rights and support

Overview

Anxiety is one of the most common mental health difficulties in children and young people. It can show up as worry, avoidance, physical symptoms (headaches, stomach aches), or difficulty concentrating. A nurturing, predictable environment and supportive relationships can help. Some learners may need specialist support from CAMHS or school-based counselling.

In the classroom

  • May avoid certain situations (e.g. speaking in front of others, tests)
  • Can appear withdrawn, irritable, or restless
  • May seek constant reassurance or perfectionism
  • Physical complaints (headaches, tummy aches) without clear cause
  • Difficulty concentrating; may seem distracted by worries
  • Absence or late arrival, especially around triggers

Suggested strategies

  • Create a calm, predictable environment with clear expectations
  • Prepare for changes and transitions in advance
  • Offer options (e.g. contributing in pairs before whole class)
  • Normalise mistakes and model resilience
  • Provide a safe space or trusted adult to go to when overwhelmed
  • Know your school’s wellbeing and mental health pathways

Further reading and CPD

  • LIAM (NHS Education Scotland) – CBT-informed anxiety intervention for CYP
  • Health and Wellbeing (GTC Scotland) – Resources for staff and learners
  • Positive Mental Wellbeing (Education Scotland) – Resources for CYP

Overview

Care experienced children and young people are those who are or have been looked after (e.g. foster care, kinship care, residential care). Many have additional support needs. Looked after children are presumed to have ASN unless assessed otherwise. The Promise sets out how Scotland should support care experienced people. Relationships and stability in school are especially important.

In the classroom

  • May have moved schools or homes multiple times
  • Can find it hard to trust adults or form attachments
  • May be distracted by home circumstances or hearings
  • Attendance and punctuality may be affected
  • May not want to draw attention to their situation
  • Homework support from home may be limited

Suggested strategies

  • Use inclusive language (e.g. "parents or carers", "take home to be signed")
  • Build trust through consistency and predictable relationships
  • Listen to the child; involve them in decisions about support
  • Know who the designated lead for care experienced learners is
  • Be flexible with deadlines and homework when appropriate
  • Never use acronyms like "LAC" to refer to a child

Further reading and CPD

  • Who Cares? Scotland Resources – Training and support for professionals
  • The Promise Scotland – Plan and resources for keeping the promise
  • Care Experienced Learners' Rights (Enquire) – Rights and support in education

Overview

Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs) are potentially traumatic events in childhood (e.g. abuse, neglect, household dysfunction). They can affect brain development, behaviour, and learning. Nurture approaches focus on building secure relationships and a supportive environment. Trauma-informed practice helps staff understand behaviour as communication and respond in ways that support regulation and belonging.

In the classroom

  • May be hypervigilant or easily triggered
  • Can struggle with emotional regulation (fight, flight, freeze)
  • May find it hard to trust adults or form relationships
  • Concentration and memory can be affected
  • Behaviour may seem unpredictable or disproportionate
  • May avoid certain situations that feel unsafe

Suggested strategies

  • Prioritise relationships; be predictable, calm, and consistent
  • Use a nurturing, trauma-informed lens when interpreting behaviour
  • Create a safe, low-stress environment; reduce surprises
  • Offer co-regulation (calm presence, not punitive reactions)
  • Give choice and control where possible
  • Know your school’s nurture and trauma-informed approaches

Further reading and CPD

  • ACEs National Action (Public Health Scotland) – National approach and evidence
  • ACEs and Trauma (SCCR) – Free learning on ACEs, trauma, and the brain
  • Health and Social Care Alliance Scotland – Lived experience and self-management
  • Nurture and Trauma-Informed Approaches (Education Scotland) – Summary of supports

Overview

Some learners need support with literacy for reasons including dyslexia, language difficulties, or other barriers. Assistive technology (text-to-speech, speech-to-text, word prediction) can help. CALL Scotland provides assessment, advice, and resources for pupils with additional support needs who need technology to access learning.

In the classroom

  • Struggle to decode text or read fluently
  • Difficulty with spelling, grammar, or organising writing
  • May avoid reading or writing tasks
  • Could benefit from accessible formats (e.g. audio, larger text)
  • May have ideas but find it hard to get them down on paper

Suggested strategies

  • Use assistive technology consistently; ensure it works in class and exams
  • Provide materials in accessible formats where needed
  • Allow alternatives to writing (e.g. verbal, mind maps, ICT)
  • Break writing into planning, drafting, editing stages
  • Use scaffolded tasks and word banks
  • Contact CALL Scotland for free advice on individual learners

Further reading and CPD

  • CALL Scotland Assessment and Support – Pupil assessment and assistive technology
  • CALL Scotland Downloads – Free resources, MyStudyBar, and more
  • CALL Scotland Communication and Literacy – Information and guidance

Overview

Young carers are under 18 (or 18 and still at school) and help look after someone who is ill, disabled, or misuses drugs or alcohol. Around 7% of young people in Scotland are young carers, often hidden. They may have less time for homework, feel tired, or miss out on social activities. Schools can help by identifying them and offering flexible, practical support.

In the classroom

  • May be tired, late, or absent
  • Homework may be incomplete or rushed
  • Can be distracted (e.g. checking phone about person they care for)
  • May be quiet, withdrawn, or reluctant to discuss home
  • Might miss parents’ evenings or after-school activities
  • May not identify as a young carer

Suggested strategies

  • Have a Young Carers Champion and promote awareness
  • Record young carers on SEEMiS so support is consistent
  • Be flexible with homework and deadlines when appropriate
  • Link with local young carers services
  • Use inclusive language (e.g. "someone at home" not always "parents")
  • Offer a trusted adult and a quiet space if needed

Further reading and CPD

  • Supporting Young Carers in Education (Education Scotland) – Full resource and PL modules
  • Carers Trust: Young Carer Support – Information and signposting
  • Young Carers in Schools Challenge Scotland – Charter, training, and resources

Overview

Poverty affects many learners in Scottish schools. It can impact attendance, attainment, health, and wellbeing. The Scottish Attainment Challenge and Pupil Equity Funding aim to close the poverty-related attainment gap. A rights-based, inclusive approach helps ensure all learners can participate and achieve. Cost of living pressures add to existing challenges for many families.

In the classroom

  • May lack resources (equipment, uniform, money for trips)
  • Homework support at home may be limited
  • Attendance can be affected by health, transport, or family circumstances
  • May experience stigma or feel different from peers
  • Stress at home can affect concentration and behaviour

Suggested strategies

  • Avoid assumptions; poverty is often hidden
  • Reduce costs (e.g. free or low-cost trips, no-cost homework)
  • Provide resources in school where possible
  • Use a nurturing, non-stigmatising approach
  • Support aspirations and positive destinations
  • Work with your school’s equity and attainment lead

Further reading and CPD

  • Poverty-Related Attainment Gap (Education Scotland) – Evidence and insights
  • Issue to Action: Cost of Living – PL resource on cost of living and learners
  • Equality and equity toolkit (Education Scotland) – Understanding equality and equity, planning and evaluation

Overview

Social, emotional and behavioural needs (SEBN) can affect how learners manage emotions, build relationships, and behave. Underlying causes may include trauma, ADHD, anxiety, or unmet needs. A relational, restorative approach focuses on understanding behaviour and building skills, rather than punishment. Whole-school ethos and consistent practice matter.

In the classroom

  • Difficulty regulating emotions; may escalate quickly
  • Struggle with friendships or conflict resolution
  • May resist or avoid tasks; appear defiant
  • Can be withdrawn or isolated
  • Behaviour may be a response to stress or unmet need

Suggested strategies

  • Use restorative approaches; focus on repair, not blame
  • Teach and model emotional literacy and regulation
  • Build positive relationships; be predictable and fair
  • Understand triggers; adapt the environment where possible
  • Use de-escalation; give time and space to regulate
  • Work with families and support services

Further reading and CPD

  • Emotions and the Brain (Education Scotland) – SCCR resources for practitioners
  • Relationships and Behaviour PL (Education Scotland) – Restorative, trauma-informed, and more
  • Scottish Centre for Conflict Resolution – Free training and resources

Overview

Speech, language and communication needs (SLCN) can affect how learners understand and use language. This can impact learning, behaviour, and relationships. Some need support with understanding (receptive), others with expressing themselves (expressive). Early identification and support matter. Speech and language therapy services work with schools to provide advice and programmes.

In the classroom

  • May struggle to follow instructions or answer questions
  • Limited vocabulary or difficulty finding words
  • May avoid speaking or contributing in class
  • Difficulty with conversation, turn-taking, or social language
  • May need visual support or simplified language

Suggested strategies

  • Use clear, simple language; check understanding
  • Pair spoken instruction with visuals
  • Give thinking time; avoid putting on the spot
  • Use visual timetables, word banks, and sentence starters
  • Follow SLT advice and programmes where in place
  • CALL Scotland can advise on augmentative communication

Further reading and CPD

  • Speech and Language UK Resource Library – Resources for educators
  • Stoke Speaks Out Resources – Communication development tools
  • CALL Scotland – Assistive tech and AAC for communication

Overview

Learners with English as an additional language (EAL) speak another language at home. Bilingualism is a strength. They may need support to develop academic English while continuing to use their home language. Progress varies; some are new to English, others are fluent in conversation but need support with curriculum vocabulary and writing.

In the classroom

  • May be quiet initially while acquiring English
  • Can understand more than they can produce (receptive before expressive)
  • May need support with subject-specific vocabulary
  • Cultural references and idioms may be unfamiliar
  • May have strong skills in their home language

Suggested strategies

  • Value and use home languages; bilingual resources help
  • Use visuals, real objects, and gestures to support meaning
  • Pre-teach key vocabulary; use word banks and glossaries
  • Pair with supportive peers; allow collaborative talk
  • Give extra time and scaffolding for written tasks
  • Avoid assuming low ability; it may be language, not cognition

Further reading and CPD

  • EAL (Education Scotland Parentzone) – Information and guidance
  • SATEAL – Scottish Association for Teaching EAL; conference and network
  • Bell Foundation Teaching Resources – EAL pedagogy and resources
